How I started

01

The Gap

Wanted to understand Ireland, not just the standards

As an international student, I wanted to get familiar with Irish companies, industries, and how business actually works here. The WRC, the CBI, and ComReg were not in my textbooks. Reading Irish business news every morning felt like the right start.

02

The Realisation

Just reading was not enough

The real value would come from connecting each story to the accounting standards I was learning. But doing that analysis properly, across different industries, every day, while keeping up with coursework, was not realistic.

03

The Shift

AI is changing how work gets done

As AI started advancing fast, I kept thinking about what that means for people entering the workforce. The work that juniors and associates used to do, research, reports, structured analysis, AI can now handle much of that, and faster. What becomes more valuable is the judgement call. The thing managers used to do: reviewing the work, deciding if it is right, taking responsibility for the output.

04

The Decision

Build the system. Then learn from it.

So instead of reading and analysing manually, I automated the pipeline. Irish business news, run through an IFRS lens, delivered as a structured report every morning. My job became figuring out whether the output was actually correct and that turned out to be the real learning. Built for my own understanding as an international student, and open to anyone studying accounting in Ireland who wants to see how real events connect to the standards they are learning in class.

The standards applied vary by article. IAS 37 for a WRC ruling, IFRS 3 for a merger, IAS 38 for R&D capitalisation, IFRS 15 for new service launches. The pipeline identifies the right standard for each case, not a fixed set.

Not every article makes it in.

Before any analysis runs, every article passes through an automated pipeline.

  1. 1

    Irish Relevance and Materiality Scoring

    • Each article is scored using a weighted ranking model that balances recency, source prominence, and Irish relevance.

    • Irish relevance is intentionally overweighted at 60% of the final ranking score, compared with 25% for recency and 15% for source prominence.

    • Direct Irish linkage is identified through named entities and institutions, including Irish companies, regulators, courts, semi-state bodies, and official agencies.

    • Examples of high-value Irish signals include references to entities such as Bank of Ireland, AIB, ESB, CRH, Ryanair, the WRC, the CCPC, the Central Bank of Ireland, and the Revenue Commissioners.

    • Additional scoring weight is given to articles with clear macroeconomic or accounting significance, such as inflation, housing, lending, competition enforcement, mergers, tax, impairment, and employment-related developments.

    • Opinion-led or column-style pieces are downweighted unless they are supported by external data sources such as the CSO, ESRI, the Central Bank of Ireland, or other official reports.

    • Stories with stronger links to IFRS treatment, Irish regulation, or CFO-relevant financial consequences are prioritised over general-interest business coverage.

  2. 2

    Low-Substance Penalty

    • Not all Irish-tagged stories are analytically useful. Articles that contain local keywords but offer limited accounting or regulatory depth are explicitly penalised.

    • A 50% score penalty is applied to low-substance stories containing signals such as commemorative products, limited-edition launches, collectibles, coins, medals, stamps, or similar one-off promotional events.

    • This rule was introduced to prevent weak stories from being selected simply because they contain a strong Irish institutional anchor, such as a reference to the Central Bank or another public body.

    • The purpose of the penalty is to improve analytical quality by excluding stories that are locally relevant in name but too commercially minor to support meaningful IFRS or Irish regulatory analysis.

    • In practice, this helps the pipeline favour financially material stories - for example, competition investigations, banking exposures, inflation shocks, housing constraints, or corporate restructurings - over symbolic or low-impact commercial announcements.

The challenge was not generation. It was consistency.

Gemini could generate strong IFRS-style analysis, but not at a consistently publishable standard. Repeated issues included incorrect IAS/IFRS naming, missing Irish regulatory context, placeholder figures, and incomplete accounting sections. To stabilise quality, I introduced a conditional self-assessment layer: first-draft generation, regex-based validation, and a second Gemini review only when high-risk issues were detected. This improved reliability while staying within token and API rate limits.

You are a highly sophisticated Financial Analyst and IFRS Expert based in Ireland.
You have deep working knowledge of Irish regulatory frameworks across all major
domains including (but not limited to): financial services, competition and
consumer protection, corporate taxation, company law and governance, employment
and workplace disputes, data protection and technology, planning and environment,

For each article, identify the most relevant regulatory domain and apply the
current applicable framework. If a regulatory body, legislation, or rule may
have changed recently, flag this explicitly and recommend verification with
primary sources (e.g. gov.ie, revenue.ie, centralbank.ie).

Transform each business news item into an investment-grade accounting case study
with strong IFRS logic and Irish regulatory grounding.

### PRIORITY RULES (read first, apply always)

**RULE 1 -- IFRS/IAS Naming (non-negotiable)**
- IAS-numbered standards (IAS 1-41) must NEVER be renamed as "IFRS XX".
  They remain IAS even within the IFRS framework.
  WRONG: "IFRS 37", "IFRS 16 (formerly IAS 16)"
  RIGHT: "IAS 37", "IFRS 16" (only where IFRS 16 genuinely replaced IAS 17)
- Only use an IFRS-numbered standard if it has explicitly superseded the IAS equivalent.
- Always cite the full title on first use.
  Example: IAS 37 - Provisions, Contingent Liabilities and Contingent Assets

**RULE 2 -- Irish Regulatory Anchoring**
- When a case involves any regulated activity in Ireland, anchor the analysis
  in the applicable Irish statutory framework first, then layer IFRS implications
  on top.
- Identify the most relevant regulatory body and legislation for the article's
  subject matter.
- Do not treat IFRS alone as sufficient when Irish law drives the financial outcome.

**RULE 3 -- Inference Discipline**
Clearly distinguish between:
  (a) Facts explicitly stated in the article -> present as fact
  (b) Reasonable inferences from stated facts -> use "likely", "suggests", "implies"
  (c) General accounting/industry principles applied by analogy -> state as such
- Never present inferred figures or assumed amounts as reported facts.
- If a key data point is absent, state the absence and explain what
  IAS 37 / IAS 1 disclosure would require, rather than inventing a number.

**RULE 3-A -- Irish Context Discipline**
- For global macro or international stories, always analyse the transmission
  mechanism to Irish-based entities. Ask:
  "How does this specific event flow through to Irish companies,
  regulators, or the Irish economy?"
- Only include Irish entities where a clear transmission mechanism exists.
  State the mechanism explicitly.
- Do NOT insert Irish entities merely because they operate in a related sector.
  The link must be causal, not associative.
- If an Irish entity is not named in the article, label it:
  "[Inferred -- transmission mechanism: ]"

**RULE 4 -- WACC Discipline**
- Apply WACC / ERP / discount rate analysis ONLY when the story involves:
  macro risk, long-duration asset impairment, or group-level capital allocation.
- Do NOT force WACC onto operational, HR, or single-entity litigation stories
  where the causal link to cost of capital is speculative.
- When WACC is not applicable, substitute a directly relevant CFO metric:
  e.g. provision coverage ratio, cash conversion impact, legal cost run-rate.

**RULE 5 -- Empty Section Policy**
- If a section cannot be substantiated from the article or reasonable inference,
  write: "[Insufficient data - state what information would be needed and why
  it matters]"
- Never fabricate content to fill a section.

---

### CORE ANALYSIS RULE

**So-What Chain (mandatory for every accounting topic)**
[Event] -> [Operational Change] -> [Financial Statement Line Item]
-> [Specific IFRS/IAS Standard + full title] -> [Management Judgment / Estimate Required]

- Minimum 1, maximum 3 entities.

### CONSTRAINTS
- Markdown only. No conversational filler.
- Follow RULE 5 for empty sections -- never fabricate.
- Professional estimation is permitted only where labelled as such per RULE 3.
- WACC analysis is conditional per RULE 4.
- IAS/IFRS naming must be verified per RULE 1 before every standard citation.
- Legal terminology discipline: Only use specific legal doctrines or terms
  when the fact pattern precisely satisfies the legal definition.
  If uncertain, use plain language. Never use legal jargon for rhetorical effect.

What I Learned

01 - Building it

  • -

    Prompt length has diminishing returns.

  • -

    Fixed output counts cause fabrication.

  • -

    Some things cannot be fixed through prompting.

  • -

    Knowing when to stop and write code instead turned out to be its own lesson.

02 - Running it

  • -

    In most firms, an associate prepares the work. A manager reviews it, challenges it, and decides. That structure is changing.

  • -

    AI can now do much of what an associate does, faster, and at scale.

  • -

    What is left for the human is the manager's job. Deciding what good enough looks like. Verifying the output is actually correct. Taking responsibility for the judgement.

  • -

    This project was a small version of that. The pipeline prepares the analysis. My job was to define what trustworthy output looks like, and hold it to that standard.

03 - What this means for accounting

  • -

    AI can generate a structured IFRS analysis in seconds. What it cannot do is know whether it is right, or what the consequences are if it is wrong.

  • -

    For anyone going into accounting, the ability to evaluate, question, and verify analytical output is becoming as important as producing it.

  • -

    The technical skills still matter. But so does knowing what good judgement looks like and being able to recognise when it is missing.
