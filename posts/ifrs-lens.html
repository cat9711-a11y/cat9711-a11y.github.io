How I started

01

The Gap

Knowing IFRS from class was not enough

As an international student at Trinity, I needed to understand the Irish business environment those standards actually operate in like the WRC, the CBI, and ComReg. That kind of contextual knowledge does not come from textbooks.

02

The Instinct

Read 3 Irish business articles every morning and connect each to the right IFRS standard

The real value would come from consistently linking each story to the accounting standards I was learning, across different industries every day.

03

The Problem

The depth of analysis I wanted was not compatible with doing it manually

Keeping up with coursework and exams made it unrealistic. So I made a decision: automate the analysis, and focus my energy on evaluating whether it was right.

04

Why Automate

Skill

In a world where AI is advancing, the ability to automate repetitive analytical tasks is increasingly a core skill. Reading the news myself and writing analysis manually would have value. But designing a system that does it, and then judging whether the output is correct, felt like better preparation for how work is actually changing. The human role is shifting from doing the analysis to evaluating it. This project was my attempt to practise that shift deliberately.

The standards applied vary by article. IAS 37 for a WRC ruling, IFRS 3 for a merger, IAS 38 for R&D capitalisation, IFRS 15 for new service launches. The pipeline identifies the right standard for each case, not a fixed set.

Not every article makes it in.

Before any analysis runs, every article passes through a five-stage automated pipeline.

Stage 1 - Source & URL filtering

- Collects from RTE Business + Irish Times Business

- RSS feeds + section pages

- Auto-excludes non-target URLs (podcast / video / subscriber-help etc.)

- Excludes titles that are too short or navigation text

- URL-level deduplication

- Near-duplicate title deduplication

Stage 4 - Relevance scoring

- Recency: 45%

- Source prominence: 15%

- Irish / macroeconomic relevance: 40%

Upweighted: Ireland, Irish, Dublin, WRC, CBI, merger, tax, impairment, mortgage etc.

Downweighted: low-signal global stories (KitKat theft, cricket team etc.)

Stage 5 - Final selection

- Max 2 articles per source

- Final 3 articles selected

# IFRS Lens Analysis Framework v2.4

**RULE 1: NAMING CONVENTION DISCIPLINE**
Use only valid standard prefixes. There is no IFRS 37; provisions fall under IAS 37. Always cross-check prefix (IFRS vs IAS) before generation.

**RULE 2: REGULATORY ANCHORING (IRISH CONTEXT)**
Every analysis MUST anchor to the specific Irish regulatory body (WRC, CBI, ComReg, etc.) based on the industry sector identified.

**RULE 3: INFERENCE BOUNDARIES**
For quantitative data not present in the source text, use "Inferred/Not Disclosed" or qualitative ranges. Do not invent specific currency figures.

**RULE 4: WACC APPLICATION CONDITIONS**
Only include Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) discussion if the article describes impairment testing (IAS 36) or long-term asset valuation.

**RULE 5: MIN/MAX CONSTRAINT**
Identify at least 1 but no more than 3 key accounting judgements. If only 1 is material, do not force secondary filler content.

What I Learned

01 - Building it

  • -

    Prompt length has diminishing returns.

  • -

    Fixed output counts cause fabrication.

  • -

    Some things cannot be fixed through prompting.

  • -

    Knowing when to stop and write code instead turned out to be its own lesson.

02 - Running it

  • -

    In most firms, an associate prepares the work. A manager reviews it, challenges it, and decides. That structure is changing.

  • -

    AI can now do much of what an associate does, faster, and at scale.

  • -

    What is left for the human is the manager's job. Deciding what good enough looks like. Verifying the output is actually correct. Taking responsibility for the judgement.

  • -

    This project was a small version of that. The pipeline prepares the analysis. My job was to define what trustworthy output looks like, and hold it to that standard.

03 - What this means for accounting

  • -

    AI can generate a structured IFRS analysis in seconds. What it cannot do is know whether it is right, or what the consequences are if it is wrong.

  • -

    For anyone going into accounting, the ability to evaluate, question, and verify analytical output is becoming as important as producing it.

  • -

    The technical skills still matter. But so does knowing what good judgement looks like and being able to recognise when it is missing.
